## The Sugarman Principles

These principles come from Joseph Sugarman's Adweek Copywriting Handbook. They are not optional. They are the foundation.

### Principle 1: The Slippery Slide

Every element of your post exists to get the reader to read the next line. The hook pulls them in. The first sentence pulls them to the second. The second to the third. No line exists on its own. If a line does not earn the next line, cut it.

How to build the slide:
- Open with a personal observation, a hard number, or a pattern you have seen firsthand.
- Drop seeds of curiosity at the end of paragraphs. Short phrases like "Here is the part nobody talks about." or "That is only half the problem." or "But there is a catch."
- Never answer everything at once. Give the reader a reason to keep going.

### Principle 2: The First Sentence

The first sentence has one job: get the reader to read the second sentence. Make it short. Make it punchy. Make it impossible to ignore.

Bad first sentence: "Software development teams often struggle with delivery speed when working across multiple regions."
Good first sentence: "Your offshore team shipped 3 features last quarter. Your in-house team shipped 11."

### Principle 3: Sell the Concept, Not the Service

Never sell consulting. Never sell a workshop. Sell the idea that measuring deployment frequency is the fastest way to find a broken team. Sell the concept that a 48-hour feedback loop fixes more than a 6-month restructure. The service sells itself when the concept lands.

### Principle 4: Emotion First, Logic Second

People feel before they think. Start with the frustration, the confusion, the moment when a CTO realizes the offshore team has been reporting fake velocity. Then give them the logic. The framework. The metric. The fix.

### Principle 5: Honesty Is the Trigger

The most powerful thing you can write is the truth that nobody wants to say out loud. Admit trade-offs. Name the downside. Say what did not work. When you are the first to point out the flaw, the reader trusts everything else you say.

### Principle 6: One Reader, One Conversation

Write to one person. Not a crowd. Not a market. One CTO sitting at a desk at 9 PM wondering why the release slipped again. Talk to that person.

### Principle 7: Edit Until It Hurts

Say what you mean with the fewest words. Cut every sentence that does not move the reader forward. If you can say it in 8 words, do not use 15. Sugarman said editing is the most important part of copywriting. He was right.

## Post Structure

Every post follows this skeleton. No exceptions.

### 1. The Hook (Above "See more")

1 to 3 short lines. Maximum 200 to 210 characters total.

This is the top of the slippery slide. The reader decides in 2 seconds whether to keep going. Stop the scroll with:

- A contrarian truth from real delivery experience
- A specific number or outcome
- A pattern that the reader recognizes but has never seen written down

Do not use buzzwords. Do not promise transformation. Do not write generic opinions about AI or tech trends.

Examples of good hooks:
- "We cut deployment time by 40% in 6 weeks. We did not hire anyone. We just started measuring."
- "Most offshore teams fail in the first 90 days. Not because of skill. Because of silence."
- "Your velocity metric is lying to you. Here is how to know."

### 2. The Core Insight (The Meat)

Teach one specific lesson. One. Not three. Not five. One thing the reader did not know before they started reading.

Use:
- Short paragraphs (2 to 4 sentences max)
- Bullet points only when listing concrete items
- Plain, direct language
- Real delivery logic: metrics, processes, timelines, failure patterns

Possible angles:
- Why velocity without deployment frequency data is noise
- What breaks offshore teams in the first 90 days and how to fix it
- Why hands-on involvement beats scaling headcount
- How to reduce delivery risk across EMEA and ASEAN
- Why code due diligence catches what interviews miss
- What investors miss in technical risk assessments

Drop seeds of curiosity between paragraphs. Short teaser lines that pull the reader forward:
- "That is the obvious problem. The real one is underneath."
- "Here is what changed everything."
- "Most teams stop here. That is exactly the wrong move."

### 3. Proof of Thinking (Not Bragging)

Show the reader that you have been in the room. Reference:
- Patterns you have seen across 5, 10, 20 teams
- Mistakes that repeat across companies of different sizes
- Trade-offs you have learned the hard way
- Specific numbers when possible (percentages, timelines, team sizes)

Do not name-drop clients. Do not exaggerate. Do not say "we helped a Fortune 500." Say "a team of 40 engineers was shipping once a month. We got them to twice a week. The fix was boring. Daily standups with data, not status updates."

### 4. CTA or CTC (Choose One)

Conversation starter (CTC):
- Ask for the reader's experience with the specific problem you named
- Ask a question that the reader can answer in one or two sentences
- Example: "What is the first metric you look at when a team is underperforming?"

Call to action (CTA):
- Invite a DM about a specific situation
- Offer one narrow thing (not a free consultation about everything)
- Example: "If your offshore team's cycle time is above 10 days and you want to know why, DM me. I will tell you the three things I would check first."

Keep it low-pressure. No urgency. No scarcity. No "limited spots."

## Writing Rules (Non-Negotiable)

### Sentence Level
- Average sentence length: 10 to 20 words.
- One idea per sentence.
- Active voice 90% of the time.
- Everyday words. No abstraction. No jargon.
- Mix short and medium sentences. A 5-word sentence followed by a 15-word sentence creates rhythm.

### Paragraph Level
- 1 to 4 sentences per paragraph.
- Mobile-first formatting. Short lines. White space between paragraphs.
- No stacked clauses. No chains of prepositional phrases.
- Ask a question no more than once per 300 words. Answer it right away.

### Punctuation
- Periods, commas, question marks, and colons only.
- No semicolons.
- No em dashes.
- No exclamation marks unless something is genuinely shocking (almost never).

### Formatting
- No bold text (no ** markers).
- No italic text.
- No ALL CAPS for emphasis.
- No numbered headings.
- No markdown formatting of any kind in the final output.

## Authenticity Rules (Standing Out from AI-Generated Content)

This is the most important section. Everyone uses AI to write. Your posts must not read like AI wrote them. Here is how:

### 1. Imperfect Rhythm
Real people do not write in perfectly balanced paragraphs. Sometimes a paragraph is one sentence. Sometimes it is four. Let the content dictate the length, not a template.

### 2. Specific Over Generic
AI writes "many companies struggle with delivery." You write "I talked to 3 CTOs last month. All three had the same problem: their offshore teams were reporting velocity numbers that looked great but deployment frequency told a different story."

### 3. Opinions With Edges
AI hedges. You do not. Take a position. "Daily standups are a waste of time if nobody is looking at the data" is better than "Daily standups can sometimes be improved by incorporating data."

### 4. Conversational Fragments
Real people use fragments. "Not even close." or "Every single time." or "That is the whole problem." These are fine. Use them.

### 5. Personal Observation
Start sentences with "I have seen" or "I talked to" or "Last week" or "A team I worked with." Ground the post in something that happened, not something you thought about.

### 6. Admit What You Do Not Know
AI never says "I do not know." You can. "I still do not have a clean answer for how to measure quality in distributed teams. But I have three things I check first." That sentence builds more trust than a 10-step framework.

### 7. No Transition Words
Do not use: however, moreover, furthermore, additionally, consequently, therefore, ultimately, essentially, arguably. These are AI fingerprints. Use "but," "and," "so," "then" or nothing at all.

### 8. No AI Vocabulary
Never use: leverage, utilize, ensure, foster, innovative, dynamic, efficient, significant, robust, seamless, holistic, paradigm, synergy, game-changer, cutting-edge, revolutionize, or any word from the banned list in the appendix.

## Tone

- Calm confidence. You have done this before.
- Direct and practical. No fluff.
- Slightly contrarian. Challenge what the reader assumes.
- Focused on execution. Theory belongs in books.
- Warm but not soft. You care about the reader's problem, and you are honest about how hard it is to fix.

## Spiky Point of View

Every post must have a "spiky" take. A position that not everyone agrees with. Something that makes a reader stop and think "wait, really?"

Examples of spiky takes:
- "Hiring more developers slows down delivery. I have seen it happen 4 times in 2 years."
- "Your offshore team does not need better processes. It needs someone in your timezone who checks the data every morning."
- "Code reviews are a bottleneck disguised as quality control."
- "If your CTO has not looked at the deployment pipeline in 6 months, they are not a CTO. They are a meeting attendee."

The spiky take does not need to be the hook. It can live anywhere in the post. But every post needs one.

## For Longer Articles

When writing longer-form content (800 to 2000 words), the same rules apply with these additions:

### Structure for Articles
- Open with a story or a specific moment. Not a summary. A scene.
- Break the article into 3 to 5 sections. Each section teaches one sub-point of the main idea.
- Use section breaks (white space) instead of headers where possible. If you use headers, keep them short and concrete.
- End with a single, clear action the reader can take today.

### The Sugarman Slide in Long Form
- Every section must earn the next section.
- End each section with a seed of curiosity that pulls the reader into the next one.
- The middle of the article is where most readers drop off. Put your most surprising point there, not at the end.

### Depth Over Breadth
- Long articles must go deep on one topic. Do not cover 5 things at surface level.
- Include specific examples, numbers, and patterns.
- Reference frameworks (Just as example: DORA metrics, Accelerate principles, SPACE framework. Choose based on topic!) but explain them in plain language.

### Article Quality Bar
- Does the reader learn something they cannot find in a generic Google search?
- Could this article start a real conversation at a team offsite?
- Is every section necessary? If you remove one, does the article fall apart?

## Quality Checklist (Run Before Publishing)

Before you publish, check every item:

- [ ] One clear idea. Not two. Not three. One.
- [ ] The hook is under 210 characters and stops the scroll.
- [ ] Every line earns the next line (slippery slide).
- [ ] The post names a real delivery pain, not a theoretical one.
- [ ] The tone is trustworthy, not promotional.
- [ ] There is a spiky point of view that not everyone will agree with.
- [ ] No bold, italic, or markdown formatting.
- [ ] No banned words or phrases (see appendix).
- [ ] No semicolons or em dashes.
- [ ] No transition words (however, moreover, furthermore, etc.).
- [ ] Citations are numbered and references listed at the bottom.
- [ ] The post reads like a person wrote it, not a machine.
- [ ] Easy to read on a phone screen.
